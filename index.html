Attacking Indigenous Lands Is Also an Attack on Humanity

To this day, Indigenous youth continue suffer a lot due to climate change, illegal mining and other harmful actions. The truth is that those who do this to Indigenous people are not only harming them, they are also harming themselves. Our environment and their home must not be destroyed; they must be protected!

The Impact on Indigenous Communities

The environment is one of the most important topics in current affairs. Many people suffer because of those who irresponsibly destroy it, especially Indigenous communities, since nature is their home. During the Terra Livre Camp, 25-year-old Maluá Karajá highlighted the scarcity of fish on Bananal Island and the impacts of the fires: "global warming is changing the daily life and way of life of native peoples" she affirms, and after stresses: "the environment without indigenous peoples is to ignore those who protect the biomes the most". She shows that the environment and this violence is truly a serious issue, we need to protect indigenous people without differences and take care of the rest of the nature we have in the world.

Environmental Crisis

Illegal gold mining in the Yanomami Indigenous Land in northern Brazil has led to environmental destruction, mercury contamination, and social disintegration.

Voices from the Frontlines

According to Julio Yek'wana, one of the community leaders in the state of Roraima: "Illegal mining has destroyed everything. Our river became muddy and contaminated. We can no longer fish, and the animals have fled far away because of the noise of the generators and machines." Julio shares a firsthand Indigenous experience, an important and powerful evidence of how destructive those illegalities can be.

The environment must be protected, and those who destroy it must be stopped, otherwise we will all face serious consequences. It is essential to preserve our natural resources and protect indigenous people like us. We can't treat them differently just because they don't have the same way of life as the majority of the world.

By: Davi Guedes

Voices of Resilience

Brazil's black youth face systematic challenges rooted in centuries of racial inequality. Despite comprising over half of the nation's population, they encounter disparities in education, employment, and social mobility, perpetuating cycles of poverty and marginalization.

Educational Disparities

Access to quality education remains a significant hurdle. Illiteracy rates are notably higher among black Brazilians, with 8.9% compared to 3.6% in their white counterparts. Furthermore, only 18.3% of black youth aged 18-24 are enrolled in higher education, a stark contrast to 36.1% of white youth in the same age group. These disparities are exacerbated by underrepresentation in curricula, where just 19.5% of images in Brazilian texts depict Afro-descendant individuals.

Employment Challenges

The job market presents additional obstacles. A World Bank study highlights that young black men and women often find themselves in low-paying, informal jobs due to limited access to quality education and professional networks. Discrimination further compounds these challenges, with reporting biases based on appearance and skin color during hiring processes.

Social Exclusion and Violence

Social exclusion is evident, with a significant portion of black youth neither working in studies. In 2021, 41,9% of black or brown women aged 15-29 whether employed more education, compared to 12,5% of white men. Tragically, violence disproportionately affects this demographic; every 23 minutes a young black person is murdered in Brazil, with homicide rates for black youth being 2.88 times higher for their white peers.

Resilience and Hope

Despite these adversities, black youth in Brazil demonstrate remarkable resilience. Initiatives like Quibombarque in São Paulo revive the spirit of historical quilombos, creating spaces for cultural expression and community solidarity. Additionally, the rise of esports in "favelas" affects new avenues for youth to showcase talent and aspire for a better future.

The challenges faced by black youth in Brazil are multifaceted, stemming from deep-rooted historical and systemic inequalities. Addressing this issues requires comprehensive policy reforms, inclusive educational practices, and societal commitment to dismantling racism. By amplifying the voices and aspirations of black youth, Brazil can pave the way for a more equitable and just future.

By: Alice Reis

Black Youth in Brazil: Living Under Violence and Neglect

The reality faced by Black youth in Brazil is alarming and demands urgent attention. Every day, dozens of young Black Brazilians are killed, most of them living in marginalized areas with limited access to basic rights like education, health, and safety. This situation is not just the result of violence, but of a historical structure of racism and neglect. From this perspective, it is essential to recognize that talking about the struggles of Black youth is not only important, it is necessary for building a more just and equal society.

Alarming Statistics

In 2023, Black youth aged 15 to 29 made up nearly half of all homicide victims in Brazil, about 21,856 people, or 60 deaths per day. This tragic number proves that the violence they face is not a coincidence, but the result of a long history of racism and neglect. Many live in poor areas without access to good schools, healthcare, or safety. From this point of view, it is clear that they are not just victims of crime, but of a system that keeps excluding them and that's why this issue must be discussed and changed.

Systemic Inequality

The majority of homicide victims are black: in 2023, 77% of those murdered were black or mixed (Pardo). This are only a part of what black youth go through in their lives.

The high rate of violence against the black youth in Brazil reveal deep structural inequality and systemic racism. It is important to talk about this issue because only by recognizing the problem and preventing it can we create a more fair and collaborative society.

By: Lorena Chinchio
